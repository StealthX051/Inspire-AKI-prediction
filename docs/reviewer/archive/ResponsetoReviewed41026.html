Comments to the Author

This manuscript details a retrospective single-center study (INSPIRE database, Seoul National University Hospital, n=49,198), using multiple ML models to predict severe (Stage 2/3) postoperative AKI in noncardiac surgery patients, comparing preoperative-only versus combined pre- and intraoperative inputs, and tabular feature-engineered models versus a hybrid MLP+LSTM. The best model (AutoGluon, combined data) achieved AUROC 0.934, though the gain from adding intraoperative data was statistically significant but clinically trivial. The manuscript is clear and well-written, and its conclusion that preoperative tabular data alone performs nearly as well as adding intraoperative time-series is well-supported, but several methodological concerns limit confidence in the absolute performance claims and generalizability.

This discrepancy or performance boost is not substantially explained, whether the outcome definition (only stage 2/3), specific data population, or overfitting contributed to the performance.

Reviewer: 2

Comments to the Author

This study addresses a clinically important question with a rigorous, robust, and honest methodology. The valuable contribution here is that optimized preoperative tabular models are not meaningfully improved by adding low-frequency intraoperative data, particularly given the current enthusiasm for time-aware deep learning in clinical prediction. I appreciate the supplemental package being thorough, as well as the inclusion of the decision curve analysis along with DeLong testing. The following concerns should be addressed before the manuscript is suitable for publication.

 

1. Unit of analysis and potential data leakage

 

The cohort table reports 49,198 patients but 60,824 operations. It is never explicitly stated whether modeling and train/test splitting were performed at the patient or operation level. If the split was operation-level without grouping by patient, the same patient's preoperative laboratory values and physiology could appear in both training and test sets, artificially inflating all reported performance metrics. The authors should clarify this explicitly in the Methods and, if the split was operation-level, provide a patient-grouped sensitivity analysis confirming that results are not materially affected.

Each operation should have different preoperative objective data associated with it, but patient level factors will be repeated. We could possibly do a sensitivity analysis (a very brief one, may not ALL the models) in the first operation for each patient who had > 1.[h] Or simply reassure them in some way.

2. Imputation of intraoperative variables with -99

 

Variables with greater than 10% missingness were imputed to -99 prior to z-score normalization. This approach, while efficient, produces extreme negative values after normalization that may create spurious split boundaries in tree-based models and distort SHAP values for those features. The authors should verify that -99-imputed features are not among the dominant SHAP contributors, or provide a brief sensitivity analysis comparing results under an alternative imputation strategy such as missing indicator encoding or median imputation with a separate missingness flag.

Same concern, see my comment in reviewer 1 section

3. The prominence of department_OS in SHAP rankings

 

The ophthalmology department indicator is the second most important feature in the GBT combined model SHAP beeswarm, ahead of ASA score and most laboratory values. Ophthalmology procedures are typically brief, elective, and performed in low-risk patients, yet the inclusion criteria exclude local anesthesia cases. The authors should explain what ophthalmology cases remain in[i] the cohort and why this department indicator carries such strong predictive weight. If it is acting as a case-mix prox[j]y rather than a true clinical signal, this has implications for the interpretability claims made in the Discussion.

What a detailed catch – can we look into where this is showing up and why? On a sidenote, I’d consider looking at what surgical services we actually have and limiting to certain ones that have a minimum of x cases, o[k]r even clinically relevant services (excluding things like anesthesiology and ophthalmology, endoscopy, etc). Needs attention here.

4. AKI outcome definition justification

 

The decision to collapse Stage 1 AKI into the negative class is reasonable on clinical grounds, but the two supporting citations (refs 20 and 21) are both ICU-based studies. Surgical cohort-specific evidence for this threshold should be provided, or the authors should acknowledge more directly that the choice limits comparability with prior perioperative AKI literature that uses all-stage definitions.

5. Cross-validation design

 

The paper describes "five iterations of randomly shuffled 5-fold cross-validation[l][m]" but does not explain why this design was chosen over a single stratified 5-fold scheme or a fixed held-out test set. With repeated random shuffles, the same observations can appear in the test fold across multiple iterations, which affects how confidence intervals should be interpreted. The rationale for this design should be stated, and the authors should confirm that patient-level overlap across folds was not introduced in this process. Agree

 

6. MLP+LSTM training parity

 

The LSTM-based models were optimized with 50 Optuna trials and up to 100 epochs per trial, while AutoGluon received a 10-minute wall-clock budget. The authors offer a compelling mechanistic explanation for the LSTM underperformance (sampling frequency mismatch), but the asymmetry in optimization resources should be acknowledged as a limitation rather than left implicit.
